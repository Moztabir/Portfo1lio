Project Overview

A playful learning platform built to make math feel less like work

Mathive focuses on developing an interactive Grade 4 math learning platform that makes numeracy more enjoyable through colorful presentation, competition, and game-like interaction. The platform is designed to support both solo learning and competitive play while keeping navigation simple and accessible for younger students.

What the project is

Mathive is a web-based platform with mini math games that reinforce addition, subtraction, multiplication, division, pattern recognition, and word problems. Students log in, choose how they want to learn, and then practice through short, interactive activities instead of static exercises.

Why I built it

A lot of kids find math boring, slow, and repetitive. The goal of Mathive was to turn that experience into something faster, more rewarding, and more engaging by making learning feel more like playing.

My Role

I built the system that made the platform actually work

I handled everything except the UI design. My main work focused on the front-end logic, gameplay functionality, leaderboard systems, data handling, and backend integration. That meant taking the concept and turning it into a working learning platform with real interaction, progression, and stored results.

What I worked on

  • Built gameplay logic for the math mini games
  • Created score tracking and timer systems
  • Integrated leaderboard functionality
  • Worked with Supabase and SQL for data storage
  • Helped structure teacher dashboard functionality

My contribution

My role was about functionality and system structure. I focused on making Mathive feel like a real product instead of just a visual concept by making sure the games, competition flow, and backend systems all connected properly.

The Problem

Math learning can feel repetitive, unmotivating, and disconnected from how kids like to learn

For many students, traditional math practice feels too static. Worksheets and routine exercises often do not provide enough energy, challenge, or immediate reward. That makes it harder to keep students engaged, especially when the subject already feels intimidating.

  • 1

    Low engagement

    Kids can lose interest quickly when math feels repetitive and visually flat. Without interaction, it becomes harder to keep their attention.

  • 2

    Not enough motivation

    Traditional exercises do not always give students a reason to push themselves. There is often no strong sense of progress, reward, or challenge.

  • 3

    Different learning preferences

    Some students prefer quiet practice while others perform better when they can compete. A good platform should support both styles without making the learning experience confusing.

The Solution

A game-based platform that turns math practice into fast, rewarding interaction

Mathive solves this by mixing core Grade 4 math skills with gameplay systems like timers, leaderboards, difficulty modes, and challenge loops. Students can either play solo at their own pace or take part in competitive experiences that make learning feel more exciting.

“The goal was to make math feel less like a worksheet and more like something students actually want to play.”

Design Goal

Build a playful, clear, and kid-friendly platform with smooth navigation, visual energy, and simple interactions that students can understand quickly.

User Goal

Help students improve math skills through short and rewarding practice sessions, while giving them a reason to stay engaged through progress, challenge, and competition.

Process

From learning concept to a working mini-game platform

The project started with the idea of making Grade 4 numeracy more interactive, then grew into a system with multiple game modes, backend support, and teacher-focused features. The process involved identifying skill areas, designing game loops, building data systems, and making sure everything felt fast and usable.

  • 1

    Defining the learning goals

    We identified the Grade 4 numeracy skills the platform should target, including arithmetic, graph reading, factoring, and measurement-based problem solving.

  • 2

    Designing the game structure

    Each mini game was planned around speed, clarity, and simple rules so students could jump in quickly without needing complex instructions.

  • 3

    Building logic and systems

    My work focused on connecting gameplay logic with scoring, timers, difficulty modes, and backend data so the experience felt complete and functional.

  • 4

    Competition and progression

    The leaderboard and dashboard ideas helped shape the platform into something more than just isolated games by adding progression, review, and comparison.

  • 5

    Iteration and improvement

    As the project developed, the platform became more focused on balancing fun and actual learning value. The challenge was making it playful without losing the educational purpose.

Mini Games

A set of quick activities built around core Grade 4 numeracy skills

The core of Mathive is a set of mini games designed to reinforce skills while keeping the experience playful and competitive.

01

Dice Addition

Players solve fast addition challenges using randomized dice values under time pressure.

02

Bar Graph Battle

Students answer graph-based questions quickly to strengthen interpretation and comparison skills.

03

Factor Smash

A quick-reaction factor game that turns multiplication and number relationships into something more active.

04

Area Attack

Players solve area-based challenges that help connect measurement concepts with interactive play.

05

Perimeter Panic

A perimeter-focused challenge designed to make problem solving feel faster and more engaging.

Lessons Learned

What the project taught me as a developer

Mathive taught me how to balance fun interaction with actual educational value. It pushed me to think about speed, logic, user flow, and backend structure all at once. More than anything, it showed me how much difference good interaction design can make when the goal is to keep users engaged.

Biggest Challenge

The hardest part was making the platform feel exciting without losing the learning purpose. The games needed to be simple enough for students to understand quickly, but still structured enough to reinforce real math skills.

What I’m Most Proud Of

I am most proud of building the systems that made the platform functional: the gameplay logic, leaderboard flow, backend support, and everything that helped Mathive feel like an actual working product rather than just an idea.

Design Learning

I learned that educational products need a lot of clarity. Even when a platform is playful, students still need quick feedback, simple navigation, and a strong visual hierarchy.

Development Learning

I improved at building interaction systems that connect front-end experiences with stored data. It helped me think more deeply about how gameplay, scoring, difficulty, and dashboards work together.

Next Steps

How Mathive could grow in the future

Mathive already creates a strong base for playful math learning, but there is room to make it even more engaging and more powerful as a full platform.

Multiplayer

A multiplayer mode could let students compete in real time and make classroom challenges even more exciting.

AI Tutor

An AI tutor could give hints, explain mistakes, and personalize support based on how each student performs.

Better Analytics

Expanded dashboard analytics could give teachers deeper insight into student strengths, weaknesses, and overall progress.
